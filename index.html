Krugman‚Äôs Hot Dog and Bun Thought Experiment

Imagine an economy that produces only two things: hot dogs and buns. Each hot dog requires exactly one bun. They are perfect complements.

Suppose both sectors employ 100 workers, each producing 100 units a day ‚Äî 10 000 hot dogs and 10 000 buns. Output is perfectly matched, labour fully employed, and wages equal across both industries.

Now imagine a technological improvement doubles productivity in the hot-dog sector. With the same 100 workers, it can produce 20 000 sausages.

At first, this seems like a triumph of innovation ‚Äî until we notice that bun production remains capped at 10 000. Since every finished product requires both components, total output of hot dogs is still limited to 10 000.

The productivity gain doesn‚Äôt increase national output; it reallocates labour value. Employment and wages fall in the automated (hot-dog) sector, while labour in the slower (bun) sector becomes more valuable. The total number of hot dogs stays the same, but income and importance shift from the fast sector to the slow one.

Krugman‚Äôs insight was elegant: when technology accelerates one part of a complementary system, the value of labour migrates to the part that hasn‚Äôt caught up yet.

The Modern Parallel: Data Scientists and AI Engineers

That simple economic pattern explains a shift many of us have quietly witnessed in the technology consulting world.

A few years ago, data scientists were the heroes of the AI revolution ‚Äî the innovators building models, training algorithms, and promising transformation. But most consultancies discovered an awkward truth: they didn‚Äôt have enough client projects to apply those models. There were more boring projects than the cool projects that Data Scientists could do. Most of Data Science projects were more proofs of concept than production systems.

Then came AI engineers ‚Äî the complementary sector. They didn‚Äôt invent intelligence; they integrated it. They wrapped APIs around foundation models, built RAG pipelines, and made AI usable for real business problems.

Almost overnight, the value of labour shifted. While data scientists focused on invention, AI engineers controlled the bottleneck to delivery. Demand, wages, and visibility followed. Just as in Krugman‚Äôs model, progress in one layer (model building) made another layer (integration) economically pivotal.

The Economics of Complementary Progress

In economics, productivity gains in one area only raise total output when complementary sectors evolve alongside.

In technology, this translates to:

Innovation doesn‚Äôt create value until integration catches up.

Every time one side accelerates ‚Äî model research, infrastructure, automation ‚Äî the other side becomes the bottleneck and temporarily gains labour value. That is why, when GenAI exploded, the ability to operationalise models suddenly became more valuable than the ability to train them.

But this balance won‚Äôt last forever. Integration is already being abstracted by orchestration frameworks, auto-evaluation tools, and no-code AI builders.

When that happens, today‚Äôs integrators will feel what yesterday‚Äôs data scientists did ‚Äî the experience of being overtaken by the next layer. The next bottleneck may move toward AI governance, verification, and human‚ÄìAI collaboration design ‚Äî areas where automation still struggles to replace human judgement.

This is not the end of jobs; it is the migration of human value. Each cycle pushes us upward: from building, to connecting, to overseeing, to designing meaning.

The Notebook of Data: A 50-Year Journey Behind TRUNCATE and VACUUM in Apache Spark

Oct 20, 2025

When researching for my upcoming book on Data Engineering, I decided to trace back the origins of some of the most ‚Äútaken-for-granted‚Äù operations in Apache Spark ‚Äî things like TRUNCATE and VACUUM.

At first glance, these commands seem mundane ‚Äî one clears a table, the other cleans up old files. But when you go down the rabbit hole, you realize they are descendants of some of the most profound computer science ideas ever invented.

This post is my attempt to tell that story ‚Äî through the metaphor of a humble notebook, and the decades of innovation that turned it into the high-performance distributed data engines we use today.

1. Writing It Down ‚Äî The Paper Notebook Era (1970s)

Mistakes? You‚Äôd cross them out and move on.

That was how early file-based databases worked ‚Äî simple, direct, and brittle.

2. The Scratch Pad ‚Äî Write-Ahead Logging (1978)

Then came Jim Gray and the idea that before writing anything to your main notebook, you should first jot it down on a scratch pad.

If you spill ink or crash mid-way, you can reconstruct everything from this pad. That principle became Write-Ahead Logging (WAL) ‚Äî one of the cornerstones of the ACID guarantees that every modern database depends on.

It was the birth of atomic and durable transactions.

3. The Magic of Versions ‚Äî MVCC (1980s)

As systems grew, multiple accountants started working on the same notebook at once. The chaos was real ‚Äî overwrites, locks, deadlocks.

Enter Michael Stonebraker and Multi-Version Concurrency Control (MVCC) in Postgres (1987).

Now, instead of locking pages, each accountant worked on their own version of a record. Readers saw a consistent snapshot, while writers created a new one.

It was the foundation for modern snapshot isolation ‚Äî what makes reads and writes peacefully coexist in PostgreSQL, Snowflake, and yes, even in Spark‚Äôs Delta Lake.

4. The Janitor ‚Äî VACUUM (1989)

With so many versions floating around, notebooks started to bloat. Enter the janitor ‚Äî the VACUUM process ‚Äî whose job was to periodically tear out old, obsolete pages once no one was reading them.

PostgreSQL introduced this officially in 1989. It kept databases lean and efficient ‚Äî and the name VACUUM stayed ever since.

5. Filing Notebooks for Scale ‚Äî Log-Structured Merge Trees (1996)

Fast forward to the 1990s. The world was scaling up. One notebook was no longer enough. We now had filing cabinets full of smaller notebooks labeled Level 0, Level 1, Level 2‚Ä¶

We learned to write fast by simply adding new notebooks ‚Äî and merge them later in batches. That was the Log-Structured Merge Tree (LSM Tree) invented by Patrick O‚ÄôNeil in 1996.

If you‚Äôve ever used Cassandra, LevelDB, or RocksDB, you‚Äôve seen this principle in action. It‚Äôs also deeply embedded in Delta‚Äôs design ‚Äî append now, compact later.

6. Distributed Accounting ‚Äî Hadoop and Spark (2010s)

The internet age turned our filing cabinet into a global warehouse. Now, hundreds of accountants (workers) each managed their own pile of notebooks (data files).

Hadoop and later Apache Spark built on this philosophy ‚Äî immutable files, distributed processing, fault tolerance. The idea of writing new notebooks instead of editing old ones became a global standard.

7. The Master Ledger ‚Äî Delta Lake (2019)

Finally, came Delta Lake, uniting all these ideas into one elegant system. A transaction log (_delta_log) acts as the master ledger, tracking which notebooks are ‚Äúactive‚Äù and which are ‚Äúarchived.‚Äù

When you run:

TRUNCATE TABLE my_table;

Delta simply adds a new entry in the ledger saying ‚Äúno active notebooks exist‚Äù ‚Äî a logical deletion.

And when you run:

VACUUM my_table;

Delta‚Äôs janitor walks into the warehouse and physically shreds the obsolete notebooks that are no longer referenced by any transaction ‚Äî a physical cleanup.

That separation ‚Äî logical vs physical deletion ‚Äî is the direct legacy of 50 years of database research.

8. The Innovation Timeline

Reflections from the Research Trail

When you look closely, Apache Spark and Delta Lake are not just new tools ‚Äî they are the latest chapters in an unbroken lineage of database evolution.

From Jim Gray‚Äôs log pad to Stonebraker‚Äôs multi-version pages, from the LSM filing cabinets to Delta‚Äôs digital ledger ‚Äî each solved the same timeless problem:

How to keep writing faster, without ever losing truth.
