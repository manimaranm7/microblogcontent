The Fixed Cost Fallacy: When ‚ÄúLooking Professional‚Äù Becomes a Liability

Many entrepreneurs‚Äîespecially in consulting‚Äîthink hiring teams or slapping ‚ÄúCFO‚Äù titles on business cards will magically attract clients. Reality check:

  • Fixed costs (salaries, swanky offices) create financial handcuffs. They demand cash even if clients vanish. Look at Carillion, the UK construction giant. They chased ¬£5B revenue with aggressive accounting and overexpansion, racking up ¬£1.5B debt. When projects stalled? Bankruptcy. Poof.[1]
  • Premature scaling kills agility. A solo consultant spending ¬£3k/month on a ‚Äúmarketing team‚Äù before securing clients? That‚Äôs like buying a Rolls-Royce to deliver groceries.
  • Growth ‚â† validation. Remember that overconfidence in growth leads to reckless spending and collapse.

Cautionary Tales: Ambition Meets Reality

  1. Wework‚Äôs Global Implosion

    • Leased posh London offices at ¬£50/sq ft before securing tenants. Result? Bankruptcy. Lesson: Fancy glass doors don‚Äôt pay bills.

  2. The Consulting ‚ÄúFloundering Syndrome‚Äù

    • Most consulting start-ups fail due to cash flow issues. Why? Many founders hire ‚Äúdelivery teams‚Äù before they‚Äôve delivered anything. Or worse‚Äîrely on 1-2 big clients. Lose one, and game over.

  3. Wise Acre‚Äôs Ice-Cold Lesson

    • Not UK, but relatable: This popsicle start-up hired 13 staff and leased a factory before securing ¬£1M funding. The 2008 crash hit, the investor backed out, and‚Ä¶ bankruptcy. Revenue first, ego later.[2]

1. Start as a ‚ÄúOne-Person Army‚Äù

  • Delay hiring until revenue justifies it.
  • Ask: ‚ÄúWill this hire directly bring in ¬£?‚Äù If not, skip. That ‚ÄúCTO‚Äù title can wait.

2. Validate Like Your Life Depends on It (Because It Does)

  • Close 5 clients manually before even thinking about a sales team.
  • Use variable costs: Co-working spaces, freelance designers, pay-as-you-go CRM tools.

3. Obsess Over Cash Flow, Not Vanity Metrics

  • Model ‚Äúworst-case‚Äù scenarios: What if 50% of clients delay payment? You can probably survive a recession by keeping at least 6 months‚Äô runway.
  • Ditch time-based billing. Switch to fixed or value-based fees.

Growth isn‚Äôt about org charts or titles‚Äîit‚Äôs about survival. As they say: ‚ÄúJaldi ka kaam Shaitan ka‚Äù (Hasty work is the devil‚Äôs work). Before scaling, ask: ‚ÄúHow will this decision put ¬£ in my pocket next month?‚Äù If the answer isn‚Äôt crystal clear, slow down.

Part 3: Offshoring Is a Business Model‚ÄîNot a Cost-Cutting Exercise

Mar 27, 2025

I‚Äôve seen it too many times.

In Part 1, I explained why consultants get laid off even when revenue is good.
In Part 2, I showed how a hybrid offshore-onsite model gives companies more time before resorting to cuts.

Now let‚Äôs talk about what many leaders get wrong‚Äîwhen and why they introduce offshoring.

Because here‚Äôs the truth:
By the time you‚Äôre scrambling to ‚Äúcut costs,‚Äù it‚Äôs already too late to build culture, delivery maturity, or trust offshore.

That‚Äôs not offshoring. That‚Äôs damage control.

The Wrong Time to Think Offshore

When someone says:

‚ÄúLet‚Äôs move some of this work offshore‚Äîit‚Äôll help our margins.‚Äù

It is usually after:

  • Bench size has ballooned
  • Utilisation is under 60%
  • Pipeline is drying up
  • The CFO is pacing

But trying to build an offshore function during a crisis is like trying to build a parachute after you‚Äôve jumped out of the plane.

What Leaders Fear (and Why They Delay)

I‚Äôve spoken to many leaders who hesitate to offshore because they worry about:

  • Losing control of delivery
  • Hiring people who don‚Äôt meet their quality bar
  • Managing across time zones and cultures

These are all valid concerns. But they‚Äôre solvable‚Äînot through micromanagement, but through conscious design.

One book that helped me early on was The Culture Map by Erin Meyer. She explains that different cultures interpret feedback, leadership, and communication in radically different ways. And misunderstanding those differences can derail delivery‚Äîeven when everyone has good intentions.

‚ÄúWhen you understand the invisible cultural wiring behind people‚Äôs actions, you can adjust your approach‚Äîwithout losing your own identity.‚Äù
‚Äî Erin Meyer, The Culture Map

That‚Äôs why offshoring isn‚Äôt a cost hack. It‚Äôs a design decision.

Offshoring Is a Strategic Design Decision

Offshoring isn‚Äôt about lowering quality. It‚Äôs not about replacing jobs.

It‚Äôs about building a resilient business model from the beginning‚Äîone that can absorb market shifts without hurting people or profits.

Here‚Äôs the uncomfortable truth:
Consulting firms don‚Äôt control client budgets.
They don‚Äôt control how long a project lasts.
They often don‚Äôt even control pricing.

But they do control how they deliver.

And when delivery cost is optimised early, you get:

  • Margin stability
  • Strategic flexibility
  • The ability to protect people‚Äîbefore it‚Äôs too late

Here is how I used High-Context Leadership to Align Cultures

When I was the Country Lead for one of the Offshore unit, we noticed something odd:
People weren‚Äôt taking holidays‚Äîeven when they had plenty of accrued leave.

It seemed like a good thing at first. But project managers started facing sudden, unplanned time-off, often due to burnout or family pressure. That made our delivery less predictable, and more risky‚Äîexactly what clients fear in offshore teams.

Digging deeper, I realised this wasn‚Äôt laziness or disorganisation. It was cultural.

In many Indian IT companies, taking holidays is still viewed as a negative. It often involves ‚Äúconvincing‚Äù a manager. So people hoarded leave, only to take it last-minute when it became unavoidable.

In one of our monthly team meetings, I explained how this behaviour‚Äîwhile understandable‚Äîclashes with the consulting business model:

  • It creates stress for project managers
  • It adds hidden risk to client delivery
  • And ironically, it damages trust in the very team trying to prove itself

That conversation changed things.
People started viewing holidays not as time-off they needed to justify, but as part of sustainable delivery planning.
Over time, this aligned the team‚Äôs rhythm with UK norms‚Äîwithout needing policy enforcement or top-down mandates.

This is what cultural alignment really looks like.
Not an HR slide deck‚Äîbut small conversations that bridge gaps with context and shift behaviours with clarity.

Ask Yourself (and Your Leaders)

‚ÄúAre we building our delivery model for resilience‚Äîor just for speed?‚Äù

If the answer is the latter, it‚Äôs a matter of time before the model collapses.
The growth trap doesn‚Äôt care how talented your people are. It only cares whether your cost structure can handle volatility.

I‚Äôve seen what happens when you build offshore too late.
I‚Äôve also seen what happens when you do it early‚Äîwith intent, with care, and with clarity.

One gives you time. The other takes it away.

This wraps up the series. But if you‚Äôre still wondering how to start designing this, or want to sense-check your team‚Äôs direction, I‚Äôm happy to chat.

Let‚Äôs build delivery models that protect both people and profit.
